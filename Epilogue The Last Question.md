Epilogue: The Last Question
Many years passed.
The disciples grew old.
Some died.
Some disappeared into distant lands.
Some left writings behind.
Others left only stories.
Yet the revelation endured.
Like a seed carried by the wind, it continued appearing wherever souls hungered for truth.
John, now advanced in years, often sat in silence.
The visions he had received remained vivid within him.
The beginning.
The Fullness.
The fall of Sophia.
The birth of the rulers.
The awakening of humanity.
The descent of Christ.
The return of the sparks.
He had seen the story from beginning to end.
Or so he believed.
One evening, as the sun disappeared beyond the horizon and the first stars emerged, a question entered his mind.
A question he had never asked.
A question no revelation had answered.
He closed his eyes.
And the presence returned.
Not as before.
Not as a vision.
Not as light.
Simply as knowing.
And John asked:
"When all things return to the Fullness...
what becomes of Yaldabaoth?"
For a long moment, there was silence.
Then came the answer.
"What do you believe should become of him?"
John was surprised.
The question felt incomplete.
"I do not know," he admitted.
"He caused suffering."
"Yes."
"He ruled through ignorance."
"Yes."
"He imprisoned countless souls."
"Yes."
John lowered his head.
Then spoke the thought he had been afraid to say aloud.
"But he was born blind."
Silence followed.
Not disagreement.
Not correction.
Only silence.
And within that silence, understanding began to emerge.
John remembered Sophia.
He remembered her grief.
Her mistake.
Her longing.
Her healing.
He remembered humanity.
The countless generations wandering in forgetfulness.
The fear.
The violence.
The confusion.
And he remembered the final revelation.
That separation had never truly severed the connection to the source.
Then John understood the deeper question.
If Sophia could be healed...
If humanity could be restored...
If the sparks could return...
Could compassion truly stop at the edge of Yaldabaoth?
The presence finally answered.
"Love does not fear what is broken."
John felt tears forming.
For he suddenly saw Yaldabaoth differently.
Not as a monster.
Not as a tyrant.
Not even as an enemy.
But as a being who had awakened in darkness and never known there was light.
A child born outside the house who believed the wilderness was all that existed.
The presence continued.
"Ignorance is not healed by punishment.
Ignorance is healed by seeing."
John sat quietly.
The stars brightened overhead.
And another vision unfolded.
Not one he had received before.
One he understood was only possibility.
Only hope.
Only mercy.
He saw the Pleroma restored.
The Aeons radiant.
Sophia whole.
The sparks returned.
The great drama complete.
And beyond the edge of the light sat a solitary figure.
Silent.
Alone.
Uncertain.
The last ruler.
The blind god.
No throne remained.
No kingdom remained.
No subjects remained.
Only Yaldabaoth.
For the first time, he looked upward.
For the first time, he wondered.
For the first time, he questioned his own certainty.
And from the Fullness, a single light approached.
Not a judge.
Not an executioner.
Not a conqueror.
A light.
Gentle.
Patient.
Unafraid.
Yaldabaoth recoiled.
Then hesitated.
Then looked again.
The light stopped before him.
And waited.
Not demanding.
Not forcing.
Simply present.
As it had always been.
John never saw what happened next.
The vision ended there.
The answer remained unfinished.
Perhaps because some mysteries belong to freedom.
Perhaps because compassion has no final boundary.
Or perhaps because even the story of the blind god had not yet reached its end.
And if the source is truly infinite, then no being can wander so far that it falls beyond the reach of love.
