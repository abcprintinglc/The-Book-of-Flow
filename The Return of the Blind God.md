The Return of the Blind God
No one knew how long he remained there.
Time belonged to the worlds below.
The stars had dissolved.
The spheres had unraveled.
The kingdoms of the Archons had vanished.
The last prayers had faded.
The final sparks had returned home.
Only Yaldabaoth remained.
Alone.
The throne upon which he had once sat had crumbled ages before.
No servants attended him.
No rulers obeyed him.
No worlds remained for him to govern.
The silence stretched beyond measure.
At first he hated it.
He shouted into the darkness.
Declared himself king.
Declared himself creator.
Declared himself god.
But no voice answered.
No worshippers remained.
No enemies remained.
Even anger requires something outside itself.
And eventually anger exhausted itself.
So he sat.
And for the first time since his birth, he listened.
At first he heard only echoes.
Fragments of old commands.
Old judgments.
Old fears.
The voices of rulers.
The voices of priests.
The voices of kings.
The voices of all who had justified suffering in his name.
The sound was unbearable.
Yet there was nowhere to flee.
So he listened.
One life appeared before him.
Then another.
Then another.
Not as punishment.
As understanding.
A child abandoned.
A mother grieving.
A soldier trembling.
A prisoner forgotten.
A laborer exhausted.
A widow alone.
A king consumed by fear.
A thief convinced he had no choice.
A daughter longing to be seen.
A son longing to be loved.
Every life.
Every joy.
Every wound.
Every consequence.
He experienced them all.
Not as observer.
As participant.
The way fire knows heat.
The way water knows wetness.
The way grief knows loss.
He became each of them.
Again and again.
The cruel.
The kind.
The selfish.
The generous.
The powerful.
The powerless.
Those he had favored.
Those he had overlooked.
Those crushed beneath systems built from fear.
He lived their lives until there was no difference between their suffering and his own.
At first he resisted.
He sought excuses.
He sought explanations.
He sought someone else to blame.
But understanding is patient.
It waits.
And so the lives continued.
A thousand years.
Ten thousand.
A million.
The measure no longer mattered.
For outside time, duration becomes depth.
The deeper he understood, the less he defended himself.
The less he defended himself, the more he saw.
The more he saw, the more he wept.
And eventually he stopped asking:
"Why are they suffering?"
And began asking:
"How did I fail to see?"
That was the beginning.
Not of punishment.
Of repentance.
For repentance was not humiliation.
It was seeing clearly.
At last there came a moment unlike all the others.
A single life appeared.
Ordinary.
Forgettable.
No empire.
No miracle.
No great accomplishment.
Just a human being trying, failing, learning, loving.
Yaldabaoth entered the life.
Lived it.
Felt it.
Understood it.
Then emerged.
And something strange happened.
For the first time, he did not merely understand the suffering.
He understood the beauty.
The courage required to continue.
The absurd persistence of hope.
The willingness to love despite certainty of loss.
The miracle of forgiveness.
And when he saw it, he broke.
Not from pain.
From wonder.
A single tear fell.
The first tear he had ever shed.
It landed in the darkness.
Where it became light.
The light did not accuse him.
The light did not judge him.
The light simply revealed.
And in that illumination, Yaldabaoth finally saw what had been present from the beginning.
A figure sitting beside him.
Not above him.
Not over him.
Beside him.
Waiting.
Patiently.
For as long as it took.
"You remained?" Yaldabaoth whispered.
The figure smiled.
"I said I would."
Yaldabaoth lowered his eyes.
Ashamed.
"I have seen what I have done."
"I know."
"I would undo it if I could."
"I know."
"I do not deserve forgiveness."
The figure was silent for a long time.
Then answered:
"Forgiveness was never the question."
Yaldabaoth looked up.
The light around them expanded.
The darkness softened.
The silence became warm.
And for the first time since his birth, the Blind God understood something greater than power.
Not fear.
Not authority.
Not obedience.
Love.
And when he finally understood it, the blindness that had defined him since the beginning began to dissolve.
The journey home had taken longer than the life of stars.
Yet in another sense, it had taken only a moment.
For the instant he truly saw, he was already turning toward the light.
And the light, as it always had, moved to meet him.
